About NMRC

A Multi-Institutional Initiative for Forest and Watershed Restoration

NMRC coordinates research, production, and applied restoration work across institutional boundaries to address one of New Mexico's most consequential ecological challenges.

Our Mission

Reforestation as Infrastructure

New Mexico's forested landscapes function as the headwaters of the state's most critical watersheds. When those forests fail — through drought-driven mortality, bark beetle, or high-severity fire — the consequences extend well beyond the treeline. Streamflows diminish, sediment loads increase, aquifer recharge declines, and the communities downstream absorb those costs for decades.

NMRC was established on the premise that reforestation is not a conservation amenity — it is water infrastructure, climate infrastructure, and community infrastructure. The Center's mission is to generate the science, build the production capacity, and coordinate the institutional partnerships needed to restore forested watersheds at the scale that New Mexico's landscape demands.

Why this matters now: Climate projections indicate that warmer, drier conditions will persist and intensify across the Southwest. Without proactive, science-guided reforestation, natural regeneration will be insufficient to restore forest cover to many post-disturbance landscapes.

💧
The Challenge

Millions of Acres. A Narrowing Window.

Forest disturbance in New Mexico has accelerated dramatically over the past two decades, outpacing the capacity for natural recovery.

Drought-Driven Mortality

Prolonged drought has weakened the defensive capacity of native conifers across the state, enabling bark beetle outbreaks of unprecedented scale. Piñon-juniper and ponderosa pine stands have experienced mortality rates not seen in the modern record.

High-Severity Wildfire

Fire behavior in overstocked, drought-stressed forests has shifted toward higher severity and larger patch sizes — conditions that eliminate seed sources and compromise the soil conditions necessary for natural regeneration.

Regeneration Failure

Even where disturbance severity allows for some residual seed source, warmer and drier post-disturbance conditions are suppressing seedling establishment. Climate-adapted planting is increasingly necessary to bridge the gap.

How We Work

From Bench Science to Landscape Outcomes

NMRC integrates the full reforestation pipeline — from ecological research and seed collection to nursery production, outplanting, and long-term monitoring. This end-to-end approach ensures that planting decisions are grounded in current science and that restoration investments are tracked for effectiveness.

1

Ecological Research

Species selection, provenance matching, drought tolerance screening, and site condition assessment.

2

Seed Collection & Genetics

Targeted seed collection from genetically diverse, locally adapted source populations across New Mexico.

3

Nursery Production

Growing climate-adapted native seedlings at scale through partner nursery infrastructure.

4

Outplanting & Site Prep

Coordinating planting operations with land management partners, applying site preparation best practices.

5

Monitoring & Adaptive Management

Long-term survival and growth tracking to build the evidence base and refine restoration methods over time.

Institutional Structure

A Shared Framework Across Institutions

NMRC is a multi-institutional initiative rather than a single-entity program. Administrative support is provided through New Mexico State University, while scientific and operational activities span a network of partner organizations.

University Research

Faculty and graduate researchers across NMSU and partner universities conduct core ecological, hydrological, and silvicultural research under the NMRC umbrella.

State Agency Partners

Coordination with the New Mexico State Forestry Division, NMDGF, NMED, and other state entities connects research outputs directly to land management programs and policy.

Federal & Tribal Partners

Active collaboration with USFS, USDA, BLM, and tribal governments ensures NMRC research addresses the full range of ownership and land management contexts present in New Mexico's forests.
