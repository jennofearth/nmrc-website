Research & Science

Applied Science for Landscape-Scale Restoration

NMRC's research program generates the ecological, physiological, and operational knowledge needed to make reforestation decisions at the scale and speed New Mexico's forests require.

Our Approach

Science That Closes the Gap Between Lab and Landscape

Reforestation at scale requires more than seedlings in the ground. It requires species and seed source selection based on current and projected climate conditions, nursery production protocols optimized for field survival, site preparation methods that give seedlings a realistic chance of establishment, and long-term monitoring that reveals what actually works.

NMRC structures its research program to address all of these needs in an integrated way — ensuring that findings flow from controlled experiments to operational guidance, and that field observations feed back into research design.

Our priority species include ponderosa pine, piñon pine, Douglas-fir, white fir, Gambel oak, and select riparian species — the backbone of New Mexico's montane and transition-zone forests.

Research Areas

Core Research Focus Areas

NMRC organizes its research around four integrated program areas, each generating science that informs the others.

01

Provenance & Species Selection

Matching seed source to planting site is one of the most consequential decisions in reforestation. NMRC conducts provenance trials at multiple sites and elevations to identify which seed sources — from which locations across the species range — produce seedlings best adapted to projected future conditions in New Mexico. This work incorporates climate envelope modeling and drought-tolerance screening to prioritize seed collections.

02

Seedling Production Physiology

Understanding how nursery growing conditions affect seedling quality and field performance is essential to scaling production reliably. NMRC research examines container type, growing media, irrigation and fertilization regimes, cold storage tolerance, and morphophysiological readiness indices for native conifers. Outcomes are translated directly into production protocols at partner nurseries.

03

Post-Fire Regeneration Ecology

High-severity fire patches present some of the most urgent and challenging reforestation contexts. NMRC investigates how burn severity, seed source distance, soil condition, and climate interact to determine natural regeneration potential — and under what conditions planting is necessary to achieve recovery. This research informs triage frameworks for prioritizing post-fire treatment areas.

04

Restoration Monitoring & Adaptive Management

Long-term monitoring of outplanting sites across New Mexico tracks survival, growth, and physiological condition over time. Monitoring data are linked to site characteristics, planting specifications, and weather records to identify predictors of success — enabling refinement of reforestation prescriptions and providing performance accountability for restoration investments.

Cross-Cutting Theme

Reforestation and Watershed Function

A defining feature of NMRC's scientific program is its explicit integration of watershed-scale hydrological research with stand-level reforestation ecology. Forest cover exerts strong controls on snowpack accumulation, snowmelt timing, evapotranspiration, streamflow, and erosion — all of which are disrupted by large-scale forest mortality and fire.

NMRC researchers quantify the hydrological consequences of forest loss and track the degree to which successful reforestation restores watershed function over time. This work generates the evidence base for framing forest restoration as water infrastructure investment — a framing that is increasingly central to how reforestation is positioned with state water policy audiences, federal funders, and municipal water utilities.

Active collaborations include work with the Rio Grande Water Fund, upper watershed municipalities, and USDA-funded hydrological monitoring networks across New Mexico and the broader Southwest.
